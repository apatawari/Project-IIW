Bacterial Vaginitis

﻿
Tweet
Email

Bacterial vaginitis refers to inflammation of the vagina. It occurs when there is a growth of bacteria. Certain bacteria are normally present in the vaginal area. When the levels of these bacteria rise, an infection can occur. The condition mostly affects women during their reproductive years, but can also occur at any time. The exact cause of bacterial vaginitis is unknown but certain factors can place you at risk for the infection. These factors include excessive douching and engaging in unprotected sexual intercourse.

 

Symptoms of Bacterial Vaginitis

The symptoms of bacterial vaginitis may include the following:

  • Itching
  • Swelling
  • Painful sexual intercourse
  • Vaginal discharge. The discharge may be thin and grayish in color
  • Unpleasant odor
  • Burning while urinating
  • Vaginal bleeding

In many cases, the infection may not produce any symptoms. It is advisable to consult a doctor in case you develop symptoms of a vaginal infection.

 

Causes of Bacterial Vaginitis

Bacterial vaginitis occurs when the microorganisms that normally live in the vagina start multiplying. Under normal circumstances, there are certain beneficial bacteria present in the vagina which keep the levels of harmful bacteria in check. However if this balance is affected it could result in an infection. Bacteria may transfer to the vagina during sexual intercourse. But bacterial vaginitis is also known to occur in women who are not sexually active. Some of the risk factors involved in the development of bacterial vaginitis include:

  • Bacterial infection may occur in women who have more than one sex partner or a new sex partner.
  • Douching can affect the balance of bacteria in the vagina and thus increases the risk of infection.
  • Use of intrauterine devices for birth control may also lead to bacterial vaginitis.
  • In some women, the levels of good bacteria in the vagina may be naturally low.

    • Bacterial vaginitis is an easily treatable condition and does not pose a serious risk. However in some cases, certain complications may occur:

  • Women with vaginal infections are at a higher risk of developing sexually transmitted diseases such as gonorrhea or HIV.
  • Women with bacterial vaginitis who also have HIV are more likely to transmit the virus to a sex partner.
  • Pregnant women with the infection may give birth prematurely or may give birth to low birth weight babies.
  • The risk of bacterial vaginitis may increase after gynecologic surgical procedures such as an abortion or hysterectomy.

You may also need to take oral antibiotic medications. However it is possible for the infection to recur and therefore it is necessary to complete the entire course of treatment. Some people may experience side effects after taking antibiotics. In some cases these antibiotics may even trigger a yeast infection. This is because most antibiotic regimens dont just eliminate the harmful bacteria; they also eliminate the beneficial bacteria.     Diet for Bacterial Vaginitis

    Here are some dietary tips for treating and preventing bacterial vaginitis:

    • The diet for bacterial infection should be low in saturated fat. Studies have found that high fat intake can lead to bacterial vaginitis and can also affect the severity of the condition. Saturated fat can diminish immunity and can also affect the natural acidity of the vagina, thereby allowing for growth of harmful bacteria. Therefore saturated fats such as butter, lard, red meat, full fat milk and biscuits should be avoided. Monounsaturated fats were shown to have a much lower effect on the incidence and severity of the infection, while polyunsaturated fats had no effect at all.
    • Vitamin E is highly beneficial for the immune system and individuals who get an adequate supply of the vitamin through food or supplements were at a lower risk for developing infections. In addition, vitamin E helps to protect you from cardiovascular disease and stroke. Vitamin E can be obtained from foods such as broccoli, spinach, collards, avocado and kiwi. Those who are unable to get sufficient vitamin E can take supplements. 100 IU of vitamin E a day is recommended. However consult your doctor before taking any supplements.
    • Folate should be an important part of the diet. It helps in protecting against bacterial vaginitis as well as several other conditions. A high intake of folate is known to help in reducing the risk of heart disease and cancer. Folate is prescribed for pregnant women to protect the unborn child from neurological defects. Folic acid also helps in boosting immunity. Some studies have shown that women with reduced folate levels are at a higher risk of developing bacterial vaginitis. Folic acid can be obtained from foods such as asparagus, avocado, spinach and breakfast cereals.
    • Drink fruit juices such as cranberry and blueberry juice. These citrus juices can help the vagina to regain its acidity and discourage the growth of bad bacteria.
    • Avoid eating foods that are rich in sugar as they can aggravate the infection.
    • Also avoid eating white breads and opt for whole grain bread instead.

    Suggestions for Bacterial Vaginitis

    Bacteria vaginitis can be prevented by following certain tips:

    • Avoid using hot tubs. To cleanse the genital area, rinse the outer area with soap and water and dry well. Use gentle soaps that are not perfumed. Also avoid using scented pads or tampons.
    • Bathing is enough to cleanse the vagina. Therefore avoid douching as this can upset the natural bacterial balance in the vagina and elevate the risk of infections.
    • Practice protected sexual intercourse by using condoms. Also avoid having multiple sexual partners.
    • Avoid consuming alcohol and smoking as they can increase your risk of developing various ailments, including bacterial vaginitis.
